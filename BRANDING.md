# Branding Policy

The **code** in this repository is free software under [AGPLv3](LICENSE). The project's
**name and visual identity** are separate — this policy explains how you may and may not
use them.

## The honest version

"Commons" is an ordinary English word. The maintainer claims **no** exclusive rights to
the word itself, and does not expect to. What this policy protects is narrower and real:
the project's **identity** — its logo, and the "Commons" name *as used to identify this
specific project* — so people can always tell the official project from a fork.

AGPLv3 grants you rights to the *code*. It does **not** grant rights to the project's name
or logo (standard — see AGPLv3 §7 and §16). Copyright and branding are different things.

## What actually protects this project

Registration isn't the point (and a common word is hard to register anyway). Protection
here comes from three practical things:

1. **The logo / wordmark.** A distinctive logo *is* protectable, even when the word isn't.
   This is the real trademark asset — treat the logo, not the word, as the thing to guard.
2. **Common-law rights in the name as used.** Simply using "Commons" to identify this
   project gives limited, unregistered (™) rights in its niche. No filing required.
3. **The fork-rename norm.** Open-source convention: fork the code freely, but rename your
   fork. Most good-faith people follow this regardless of what's strictly enforceable.

If the project ever needs a stronger, registrable mark, the path is a **distinctive logo**
or a **distinctive brand name** — not the bare word "Commons."

## What you may do

- Use, modify, self-host, and redistribute the code under AGPLv3.
- Truthfully state that your product is "built on Commons" or "based on Commons", provided
  you don't imply endorsement or official status.

## What you may not do without permission

The line is **impersonation**, not use of a common word:

- Use the name (or a confusingly similar one) *together with the project's branding/logo*
  in a way that implies your fork **is the official Commons project or is endorsed by it**.
- Present your fork as "the official Commons" or otherwise pass your version off as ours.
- Use the logo or wordmark in a way that suggests affiliation or endorsement.

You are free to run your own fork — just under **your own name and branding**. You may
truthfully say it is "based on Commons."

## Why this exists

So the communities who rely on the project can always tell the official project from a
fork — and so the code can stay freely forkable without the identity getting muddied.

Questions about branding use: open an issue or contact the maintainer.
